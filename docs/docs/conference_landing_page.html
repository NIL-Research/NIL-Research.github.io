Welcome Message

2025 Conference "Image Neuroscience and the Arts"

The emerging discipline of Image Neuroscience represents a frontier of scientific inquiry into the neural basis of aesthetic experience, creative expression, and visual perception. By employing advanced neuroimaging technologies to decode the brain's response to artistic stimuli, this field is transforming our understanding of one of humanity's most fundamental attributes: the capacity to create and appreciate art. This progress arrives at a critical time, as the intersections of cognitive science, digital media, and the humanities are increasingly recognized as vital to comprehending human cognition in its fullest complexity.

On the occasion of its inaugural international congress, under the theme "Image Neuroscience and the Arts," leading neuroscientists, artists, theorists, and curators will gather to present groundbreaking work. The congress will facilitate a rigorous examination of topics including the neural correlates of aesthetic judgment, the creative process as a brain state, the impact of digital art on neural plasticity, and the application of these insights in therapeutic and technological contexts.

Invited Participants

Semir Zeki

University College London, Cell and Developmental Biology

Professor Semir Zeki is a pioneering neurobiologist and founder of the field of neuroaesthetics. His work explores the neural basis of aesthetic experience, focusing on how the brain perceives beauty in art and music. He is a Fellow of the Royal Society and has authored several influential books on the intersection of art and the brain.

Abstract: Neurobiology and the Problem of Aesthetics

This talk will explore the neural correlates of aesthetic experience, addressing the long-standing question of whether a common neural mechanism is engaged when we perceive beauty, regardless of its source. By presenting data from cross-cultural studies, the talk will demonstrate that the medial orbito-frontal cortex (A1mOFC) is consistently activated during experiences of beauty, offering a neurobiological solution to a problem once confined to the humanities.

Jacek Rogala

Nencki Institute of Experimental Biology

Dr. Jacek Rogala is a neurobiologist specializing in cognitive and behavioral neuroscience. His research centers on the practical application of EEG and fMRI neurofeedback. A secondary line of inquiry is neuroaesthetics, where he investigates how artistic intent is encoded, transmitted, and perceived by the brain, advocating for interdisciplinary collaboration.

Abstract: Gender-Based Variations in Brain Temperature and Cognitive Performance

This presentation will discuss a study on gender differences in cognitive functioning, using magnetic resonance thermometry and fMRI during working memory tasks. The findings reveal that while males show a decrease in brain temperature during these tasks, females do not. The study suggests that females compensate for heightened temperature sensitivity by activating additional neuronal networks, highlighting the complex interplay between brain physiology and cognitive processing.
