Welcome Message

The emerging discipline of Image Neuroscience represents a frontier of scientific inquiry into the neural basis of aesthetic experience, creative expression, and visual perception. By employing advanced neuroimaging technologies to decode the brain's response to artistic stimuli, this field is transforming our understanding of one of humanity's most fundamental attributes: the capacity to create and appreciate art.

On the occasion of its inaugural international congress, under the theme "Image Neuroscience and the Arts," leading neuroscientists, artists, theorists, and curators will gather to present groundbreaking work. The congress will facilitate a rigorous examination of topics including the neural correlates of aesthetic judgment, the creative process as a brain state, the impact of digital art on neural plasticity, and the application of these insights in therapeutic and technological contexts.

We extend our sincere appreciation to the Scientific Program Committee and our partner institutions for their dedication in crafting a program of exceptional depth and breadth. To all attendees—your participation is essential to forging the new conceptual frameworks and collaborative partnerships that will define the future of this field.

Keynote Speakers

Semir Zeki

University College London, Cell and Developmental Biology

Professor Semir Zeki is a pioneering neurobiologist and founder of the field of neuroaesthetics. His work explores the neural basis of aesthetic experience, focusing on how the brain perceives beauty in art and music.

Neurobiology and the Problem of Aesthetics

Exploring the neural correlates of aesthetic experience and the medial orbito-frontal cortex activation during experiences of beauty.

Jacek Rogala

Nencki Institute of Experimental Biology

Dr. Jacek Rogala is a neurobiologist specializing in cognitive and behavioral neuroscience. His research centers on the practical application of EEG and fMRI neurofeedback.

Gender-Based Variations in Brain Temperature and Cognitive Performance

Discussing gender differences in cognitive functioning using magnetic resonance thermometry and fMRI during working memory tasks.
